Sattriya

The Neo-Classical Dance of Assam

Discover the spiritual dance tradition born in the monasteries of Assam, blending devotion, storytelling, and the rich cultural heritage of Northeast India.



Introduction to Sattriya

Sattriya is a classical Indian dance form from Assam, originating in the 15th century. It was traditionally performed in Sattras (Vaishnavite monasteries) and is deeply rooted in spiritual and religious traditions, making it the youngest of the eight recognized classical dance forms of India.

Essence of Sattriya: A devotional art form blending dance, drama, and music to narrate stories of Krishna and Vaishnavite saints.

Historical Background

Srimanta Sankardeva's Vision

Sattriya was created by the great Assamese saint-scholar Srimanta Sankardeva in the 15th century as part of his neo-Vaishnavite movement. He used dance, drama, and music as tools for spiritual education and social reform.

For over 500 years, Sattriya was exclusively performed within the walls of Sattras (monasteries) by male monks called Bhokots. This tradition helped preserve the dance form in its purest spiritual context.

Distinctive Features of Sattriya

Monastic Traditions

Originated and preserved in Vaishnavite monasteries (Sattras) of Assam.

Devotional Themes

Focuses on Vaishnavite philosophy, stories of Krishna, and spiritual devotion.

Music & Rhythm

Accompanied by Borgeet (devotional songs) and traditional Assamese instruments like khol, taal, and flute.

Masks & Costumes

Use of elaborate masks and traditional Assamese silk costumes for dramatic effect.

Masculine & Feminine Styles

Includes both vigorous masculine (Paurashik Bhangi) and graceful feminine (Stri Bhangi) movements.

Storytelling

Combines dance, drama, and expressive abhinaya to narrate mythological tales.

Costume, Music & Aesthetics





Traditional Attire

Sattriya dancers wear traditional Assamese silk costumes, often in white and yellow, with elaborate jewelry and headgear. Masks are used for certain dramatic roles.

  • Silk Costumes: Muga or Pat silk with regional motifs
  • Mukha: Masks for mythological characters
  • Jewelry: Assamese traditional ornaments
Musical Accompaniment

Sattriya is performed to Borgeet (devotional songs) and traditional instruments:

  • Khol: Main percussion instrument
  • Taal: Cymbals
  • Flute: Melodic support
  • Borgeet: Devotional singing

Learning Path for Sattriya

Learning Sattriya involves understanding both its technical aspects and spiritual significance. The training traditionally begins with physical conditioning and gradually incorporates the philosophical elements.

Foundation Level

Basic Mati Akhara (ground exercises), mudras, rhythmic patterns, and devotional understanding.

Duration: 1-2 years
Basic repertoire
Advanced Level

Abhinaya, storytelling, group choreography, spiritual expression, and performance repertoire.

Duration: 2-3 years
Performance ready
